TESTIMONY

Testimony by Eric Lane, Executive Director of the 1989 Charter Revision Commission, on the origins and potential fixes for member deference

1:01:13

·

8 min

Eric Lane, who served as Executive Director and Counsel to the 1989 Charter Revision Commission, discusses the origins of the City Council's role in land use approvals and the practice of member deference.

He explains that the original intent was to limit Council review to rare instances, but political compromise led to the current system allowing automatic review (call-up) of City Planning Commission decisions.

Lane notes that while early Council leadership prevented member deference, it eventually emerged due to member pressure, and suggests that removing the automatic call-up provision could be a legally and politically viable way to address the issue.

  • Lane takes responsibility for the current council deference mechanism, explaining it resulted from political compromise in 1989.
  • The original plan ('triple no') intended much more limited council involvement in project-specific land use decisions.
  • Speaker Peter Vallone initially prevented member deference, but the practice emerged later under member pressure.
  • Lane suggests removing the council's automatic call-up power as a potential charter change to curb deference.
  • He believes fixing deference this way is more politically feasible than significantly altering community board roles.
